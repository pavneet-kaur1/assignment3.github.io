Habitat

Red foxes are widespread. Their range extends across nearly 70 million km2 (27 million square miles), including as far north as the Arctic Circle. They can be found all across Europe, in Africa north of the Sahara Desert, throughout Asia except for extreme Southeast Asia, and across North America.The bacterium is absent from most of the southwestern United States and Mexico, as well as the Arctic islands, the most northern parts of central Siberia, and the most extreme deserts. It is not found in New Zealand, and is considered a "prohibited new organism" by the New Zealand government.In 2012, it was estimated that there were more than 7.2 million red foxes in Australia, with a range that spanned the majority of the continent.  They were first introduced to Australia in the 1830s and 1840s by settlers from the British colonies of Van Diemen's Land (as early as 1833) and the Port Phillip District of New South Wales (as early as 1845) who sought to promote the traditional English sport of fox hunting. On the island of Tasmania, a permanent red fox population did not emerge, and it is usually assumed that foxes were outcompeted by the Tasmanian devil.  However, on the mainland, the species thrived as an apex predator.


Language

Red fox body language consists of ear, tail, and postural movements, and body language emphasizes specific gestures. Postures can be divided into aggressive / dominant and fear / obedient categories. Some postures allow the two to be mixed together. A curious fox spins and snaps its ears as it sniffs. A playful person will stab his ears and stand up on his hind legs. Male foxes that are courting females or have succeeded in warding off intruders have their ears facing outwards, their tails raised horizontally, and their tips pointing up. When frightened, the red fox obediently grins, arching his back, arching his body, crouching his legs, moving his tail back and forth, and pressing his ears back against his skull. If you just submit to the dominant animal, you will have similar postures, but you will not bend your back or body. Obedient foxes approach the dominant animal in a low position, and their noses reach out with greetings.
Red foxes have a broad vocal range, producing a variety of sounds that span five octaves and blend into one another. Adults create 12 different noises, while kits produce 8 different sounds, according to recent research. "Contact" and "interaction" calls make up the vast majority of noises. The former is affected by the distance between people, whereas the latter is affected by the level of violence. One of the most common vocalizations of the fox is raspy bark.Science believe that foxes use this barking sound to identify themselves and communicate with each other.

Behaviour

Red foxes either develop stable home ranges within certain locations or are nomadic, with no permanent residence. They mark their territories using their urine. A male fox squats down and sprays urine in the ground between his hind legs, whereas a female fox squats down and sprays urine in the ground between her hind legs. Urine is also used to mark empty cache locations, which can be used to store found food, as well as to serve as a reminder not to waste time investigating them. They can precisely adjust the position of the smell imprint by using up to 12 distinct urinating postures.Family groups of red foxes share a common territory. Subordinate foxes may be present in a range in favourable habitats and/or places with little hunting pressure. Subordinate foxes can range in number from one to eight in a single region. These subordinates may have been formerly dominant animals, but they are generally young from the previous year who assist the breeding vixen in rearing her kits. Alternatively, their appearance has been explained as a result of temporary food surpluses that have little to do with assisting reproductive success.

Diet

Red foxes are omnivores who eat a wide variety of foods. Red foxes were found to consume over 300 animal species and a few dozen plant species in the former Soviet Union, according to research. Voles, mice, ground squirrels, hamsters, gerbils, woodchucks, pocket gophers, and deer mice are among the small rodents they eat. Birds (mostly Passeriformes, Galliformes, and ducks), leporids, porcupines, raccoons, opossums, reptiles, insects, other invertebrates, and flotsam are examples of secondary prey (marine mammals, fish and echinoderms).Foxes have been known to attack young or small ungulates on rare instances. They usually prey on mammals weighing up to 3.5 kg (7.7 lb) and need 500 grammes (18 oz) of food every day. Red foxes eat plants readily, and in some locations, fruit can account for up to 100% of their diet in the autumn. Blueberries, blackberries, raspberries, cherries, persimmons, mulberries, apples, plums, grapes, and acorns are all popular fruits. Grass, sedges, and tubers are examples of other plant
Predation of game and song birds, hares, rabbits, muskrats, and young ungulates has been linked to red foxes, notably in preserves, reserves, and hunting farms where ground-nesting birds are protected and raised, as well as on poultry farms.

Uses

The fur trade harvests red foxes as one of the most important fur-bearing animals. Trimmings, scarfs, muffs, jackets, and coats are made from their pelts. Cloth jackets and fur clothing, particularly evening wraps, are commonly trimmed with them. Silver fox pelts are popular for capes, while cross fox pelts are primarily used for scarves and only seldom for trimming. The entire number of fox scarves sold outnumbers the total number of scarves created from other fur-bearing animals. However, the overall number of red fox pelts utilised for trimming purposes dwarfs this figure.Furriers place the highest importance on silver colour morphs, followed by cross colour morphs and red colour morphs, in that order. In the early 1900s, the United Kingdom imported around 1,000 American red fox skins per year, while Germany and Russia exported 500,000. In 1985–86, 1,543,995 pelts were traded globally by wild red foxes. Red foxes accounted for 45 percent of the $50 million in wild-caught pelts in the United States. Pelt prices are rising, with average North American wholesale auction prices of $39 in 2012 and $65.78 in 2013.
